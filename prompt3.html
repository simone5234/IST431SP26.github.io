Prompt 3

In what ways does this technology automate social processes?

The idea of a social process being automated seems like a difficult thing to create with technology. It's hard to replicate how social processes naturally happen, but technology is able to add on to that and possibly ease how accessible it is to make social processes happen. David Autor and James Manyika write in their article on automation about how there are two types of tools: one is about closed systems that do the job on their own, while the other is about collaboration tools that require human engagement. Looking at what the definition of a social process really is, UNESCO describes it as a "pattern of interaction and relationships that occur among individuals and groups within society." University of Lucknow's assistant professor, Dr. Priya, also writes that they are social interactions between individuals and groups that occur repeatedly and are used to establish social relationships. Referring to both of those definitions, Roblox doesn't necessarily "automate" social processes, but they make it much easier for them to happen. Maybe you decide to hop on a game one day, and you meet someone you really click with, and you keep playing with them because you've discovered you have something in common. Or possibly you make a new friend at school and discover you both like Roblox, so it becomes your new online hangout spot. It's happened to me many times before, and I've made online friends on Roblox as well as other platforms, and they've become some of my closest friends. Roblox can be looked at as a tool in this sense, where it's used as a way to make connections and help social processes happen. It doesn't make them "automatic" necessarily, but it helps to bridge the potential gap that people might struggle with.

In what ways does this technology support collaboration between humans and machines?

Roblox is very supportive of the ability for users to get into game development as well as digital creation. They've advanced a lot more with their purchasable items for avatar customization, and it allows users to create items for one another. Their option of making games in Roblox Studio is also a feature of the technology collaboration they're trying to support. There are so many things that they allow users to do on Roblox with development and coding, and it shows they want people of all ages to give it a try. In one of their help pages, they talk about how many people have been able to build a career from developing games on Roblox, as well as how they have seen people "build their STEM skills" through developing on Roblox. They have also tried to make Roblox user-friendly for those who might not have as much experience or are on the younger side, because they want to encourage people to try their platform. They mention as well on the help page how parents can support and protect their child while still helping them branch out and get into game development if that's something they want to look into.

How much does this use of technology reflect technochauvinism?

I personally don't think that Roblox is a complete reflection of technochauvinism. I do believe that it can play a part in the overall theory Meredith Broussard developed, which she spoke about in her book, but I wouldn't say that the usage is a true reflection. She talks about how the definition of it is "the assumption that computers are superior to people, or that a technological solution is superior to any other." I do think that the idea of a technological solution being superior to any other solution could play a role in the usage of Roblox reflecting technochauvinism, because a lot of their solutions to possible problems are with technology. It is an online platform, so they kind of have to resort to it, but I don't think their mindset as developers is that technology is the only solution.

The way she speaks on technochauvinism in the article we read, with the lawn mower example and about the "human-in-the-loop" systems, reminds me of the overall process for Roblox's chatting system. It is conducted essentially using an AI system that filters through all of the chat messages, but they are trained by humans to be able to label what data is ok and what isn't. The way they check if any uploaded assets are appropriate is also through human moderators and machine detection to ensure it is ok for public use. There are a lot of uses in Roblox's content moderation practices where they primarily use AI or any automatic systems, but being able to integrate the human element helps to keep them on track and keep technology as a system approach rather than just a tool.

How could this technology be applied in a way that is more systems-oriented?

One quote from Val Dusek's chapter on the system's approach really seemed to embody the concept of how Roblox is more systems-oriented. He writes that the "individual is not outside the system, but inside the system," which I feel is really a great way to describe Roblox because there is so much integration with it now. At least compared to other games and possibly other gaming platforms, there is a lot more human-based integration, where it seems like Roblox is less of a tool. His mentioning of how the systems approach will make "technology encompass the humans" can relate to a lot of Roblox's features as well.

Looking at some of the gambling aspects of Roblox, as well as the different forms of advertising and purchasing methods, can suck a user in to the point that they're much more a part of the system. It's no longer really about the user being "outside of the tool" and controlling it when there is a lot more consistent usage, and the hunt for dopamine is one of the things drawing them back into Roblox or the constant itch for some type of satisfaction. It's no longer really about the fun of using Roblox since it's possibly turned into something more on the side of screen addiction. There are various ways game developers integrate gambling and advertisements into different games in a way that is fun for players. Gambling is made to be more child-friendly so that it isn't necessarily labeled as gambling, and it is allowed for underage users. There are also various advertisements for different things, but made in ways that are fun and exciting, and are easily used to advertise their products.

The University of Sydney dives into how some games have integrated loot boxes, which have always been an issue in the world of video games. They talk about how there was a ban on loot boxes for users under 15, but it's still available on Roblox and features "monetised random reward mechanics." It's fun for the players, and it is technically just a game, but it does involve them seeing something that potentially provides them with a reward as long as they keep trying, which isn't good for kids to be around, as it is just essentially gambling. Especially if it ends up costing Robux and leads to them begging their parents to spend more. Dusek mentions advertisements and seduction with the technology systems approach in his book. He writes that "technological systems that include advertising, propaganda, and government enforcement can persuade, seduce, or force users to accept them. The article by the University of Sydney also specifically mentions the games Adopt Me!, Blox Fruits, and Pet Simulator 99 to have chance-based rewards that require money to get. They also mention how some of the children interviewed, who were 11 and 13, didn't fully grasp the concept of the Robux to Australian dollars conversion, which leads to the idea that they shouldn't be around that sort of spending because they don't know how much they're really spending in-game either. Duesk's mentioning of it "persuading" users to accept a systems approach shows that popular games such as Pet Simulator 99, which has had over 2.4 billion visits, can ease people, and especially young children, into the idea that what they're doing is ok when it might not be.
